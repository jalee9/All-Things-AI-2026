# Major League Hacking AI Roadshow at All Things AI 2026
## 👩‍🏫 Key Takeaways & Notes for Faculty

## Context
The MLH AI Roadshow session provided insight into how technical skill development is evolving in the age of AI. The session challenged traditional assumptions about how students learn, what skills matter most, and how workforce readiness is defined. It emphasized experiential learning, AI-augmented workflows, and the growing importance of creativity, adaptability, and real-world application.

---

## 💡 Shift from Content Delivery to Experiential Learning
- “You can’t learn the skills to become a software engineer in the classroom”
- Emphasis is shifting toward:
  - Project-based learning  
  - Iteration and experimentation  
  - Creation over consumption  

**Implication:**  
Curriculum should prioritize **applied, hands-on learning experiences** over passive instruction.

---

## ⚡ Reduced Technical Barriers → Elevated Cognitive Skills
- “When the cost of writing code is effectively zero, what matters are ideas & execution”
- AI reduces barriers such as:
  - Syntax knowledge  
  - Tool complexity  
  - Time to prototype  

**Implication:**  
Assessment should shift toward:
- Problem framing  
- Creativity  
- Decision-making  
- Iterative refinement  

---

## 🤖 Developers as “Managers of Machines”
- “Everyone starts as a manager now—we just manage machines instead of people”
- Role evolution:
  - From writing code → orchestrating systems  
  - From syntax expertise → workflow design  

**Implication:**  
Students must develop:
- Prompt engineering skills  
- Critical evaluation of AI output  
- Iterative refinement strategies  

---

## 🚀 Hackathons as a Model for Workforce Readiness
Hackathons cultivate durable skills:

- Written communication  
- Presentation skills  
- Ideation and brainstorming  
- Learning agility  
- Adaptability  

**Implication:**  
Instruction should integrate:
- Time-bound challenges  
- Open-ended problem solving  
- Collaborative and reflective activities  

---

## 📊 Democratization of Development
- ~27 million software engineers globally  
- ~1 billion potential developers enabled by AI  

**Implication:**  
Increased access requires:
- Stronger emphasis on **ethical AI use**  
- Development of **critical thinking skills**  
- Clear guidance on **responsible tool use**

---

## 🔁 Continuous, Experiential Learning is Essential
- “When technology changes by the minute, the only way to learn is by doing”

**Implication:**  
Supports:
- Bichronous learning models  
- Lab-based exploration  
- Iterative project submissions  

---

## 🎯 Evolving Signals of Student Readiness
- “A track record of using AI to solve problems is a cheat code right now”

**Implication:**  
Programs should emphasize:
- Portfolio-based assessment  
- Real-world problem solving  
- AI-integrated project work  

---

## ⚠️ Reframing Traditional Indicators of Preparedness
The session challenges assumptions such as:
- Degree = readiness  
- LeetCode = skill  
- Internship = capability  

**Implication:**  
Institutions may need to reconsider:
- Assessment strategies  
- Credentialing models  
- Definitions of workforce readiness  

---

## 🔗 Alignment with Emerging Teaching Practices
- AI as a **co-creator, not a replacement**
- Emphasis on **human-centered learning design**
- Need for **intentional AI integration into coursework**
